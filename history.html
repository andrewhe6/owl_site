History and Origins

The Pueo is thought to have arrived sometime after the arrival of the Polynesians around 300 AD, however the exact date is unknown and some speculate they arrived earlier. The species was first named by Andrew Bloxam in 1825, who named it Strix sandwichensis. Today, the Pueo is now considered a subspecies of the more common short-eared owl, though some disagree and do not consider it a distinct species. They were widespread around the end of the 19th century, but has declined ever since, likely due to the introduction of the Barn Owl.

The Barn Owl was introduced in 1958 by the Board of Agriculture in order to control the rodent population. The birds were imported from California and 15 were initially released at Kukuihaele on Hawai'i Island. Within the next few years, 71 more were released to the other islands. In the 1990s, some Barn Owls were given transmitters to allow for movement pattern tracking. They were about as effective at controlling rodents as mongoose since they mainly ate insects. Barn Owls instead competes with the Pueo for food.

Not much is known about the Stilt-Owl due to its extinction. Human settlement and the introduction of pigs and rats are thought to be the primary factor for its extinction because their terrestrial nature made them more vulnerable to the invasive species.

Culture and Other Fun Facts

  • In Hawaiian mythology, Owls are said to rescue lost souls from the underworld and guide armies to safety
  • It is believed that the god Kāne took the form of an owl during battle
  • In the Kumulipo (Hawaiian creation chant), the Pueo owl, along with the 'Io hawk, the Nēnē goose, and other birds, were all said to be born in the third wā (third period of creation)
  • Because of their appearance and silent flying, Barn Owls have nicknames such as ghost owls, night owl, and death owl, among more
  • The Pueo, Barn Owl, and Stilt-Owl all seem to serve as design inspirations for the Pokémon Rowlet and its evolutions from the Hawai'i-inspired Alola Region
  • The Pueo was thought to be one of the physical forms that an 'aumākua (ancestor spirit) would take
  • In Hinduism, the Barn Owl is considered the be the mount and the symbol of Lakshmi, the goddess of prosperity and fortune
